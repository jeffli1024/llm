01 The Problem

Language models don't read text — they read numbers. Every word, syllable, or character must be converted into a token ID before the model can process it. But how you split text into tokens matters enormously.

❌ Character-level
unhappiness

11 tokens — too many, loses word structure, huge sequences

❌ Word-level
unhappiness [UNK]

Unknown words → [UNK]. Needs millions of vocab entries for all forms

✅ BPE subword — the sweet spot
un happiness

2 meaningful pieces. Unknown words are decomposed into known parts. Compact yet expressive. This is what GPT, Claude, and every modern LLM uses.

02 The Algorithm

BPE is beautifully simple: repeatedly merge the most frequent pair of adjacent tokens until you reach your desired vocabulary size. That's it.

1

Start with characters

Split all training text into individual characters (+ a special end-of-word marker like Ġ or ). Every character is in your starting vocabulary.

2

Count all adjacent pairs

Scan the entire corpus and count how often each consecutive pair of tokens appears together. "t h" appearing 1000 times is a good merge candidate.

3

Merge the most frequent pair

Take the top pair and merge them into one new token everywhere they appear. Add the new token to your vocabulary. Repeat from step 2.

4

Stop at vocab size

Keep merging until you hit your target vocabulary size (e.g., 50,000 tokens). The final merge rules are saved and used for tokenizing new text at inference time.

Byte-level BPE (used by GPT-2 onwards) is a key innovation: instead of starting from characters, you start from the 256 raw bytes. This guarantees zero unknown tokens — any text, in any language, can always be represented because every byte exists in the base vocabulary.

BPE was originally a text compression algorithm (Philip Gage, 1994) and adapted for NLP by Rico Sennrich et al. in 2016 — now powering every major LLM.
